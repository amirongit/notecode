# Algebra 1 Full Course
variables: symbols, generally in the form of letters, used to represent unknown quantities.<br>
factors: known or unknown quantities in any form multiplied by each other to make up other quantities.<br>
terms: multiplications of factors.<br>
coefficients: values within terms multiplied by variables.<br>
constant terms: don't have variables within their factors.<br>
expressions: combinations of terms.<br>
sub expressions: expressions within terms grouped by parentheses or brackets as one of their factors.<br>
like terms: terms sharing the same set of variable factors raised to the same exponents; can be merged together.

equations: assertions that two expressions evalute to the same value.<br>
- identities: true for all substitutions; their sides are different versions of each other
- contradictions: false for all substitutions; they don't have any solution
- conditional: true for some & false for other substitutions

inequations: assertions that two expressions don't evalute to the same value.<br>
solutions of equations or inequations are substitutes for their variables proving their assertion.<br>

least common multiple (LCM): smallest positive integer divisible by given terms (variables are treated as prime factors).<br>
least common denominator (LCD): LCM of denominators of given fractions.<br>

`(a / b) * (b / a) = 1`<br>
`a * 1 = a`<br>

interval notations are used to capture ranges of real numbers

solutions of inequations are usually written using interval notations.

- their solutions are numbers
- each of them has one solution

ratios
- they are comparisons between two quantities (`a` & `b`)
- they can be written as fractions (`a / b`) or `a : b`

rates: ratios which their terms are in different units.<br>
proportions: assertions that two ratios are equal.

`a * d = b * c` where `(a / b) = (c / d)`.<br>

ordered pairs: pair of terms (components) within parentheses in the form of `(x, y)`.<br>
cartesian coordinate systems (the cartesian chart)
- they are combinations of two perpendicular line numbers
- their horizontal line number is called x-axis
- their vertical line number is called y-axis
- intersection of their axes is called origin point

ordered pairs can be represented on cartesian charts with their corresponding components.

first degree linear equations or inequations in two variables (`x` & `y`)
- their overall form is `ax + by <=> c` where `a >= 0`
- `a` & `b` aren't equal to `0` at the same time
- their solutions are in the form of ordered pairs
- each of them has infinite number of solutions

set of all solutions of first degree linear equations or inequations can be represented as lines (or graphs) on the cartesian chart
- if `b = 0`, the equation will have fixed values as `x` & its graph move in parallel to y-axis
- if `a = 0`, the equation will have fixed values as `y` & its graph move in parallel to x-axis

x-intercept: intersection of graphs with x-axis.<br>
y-intercept: intersection of graphs with y-axis.<br>

slope: ratio of vertical to horizontal changes of graphs on the cartesian chart.<br>

given two ordered pairs of solutions of first degree linear equations in two variables
- `m = delta(y) / delta(x)` where `m` is slope of the equation
- if `m > 0`, the graph will rise moving toward right
- if `m < 0`, the graph will fall moving toward right
- if `m = 0`, the line will move in parallel to x-axis
- if `m` is undefined (`delta(x) = 0`), the line will move in parallel to y-axis

steps to solve first degree linear equations in two variables
1. one random value is picked as either of the variables
    - if `c = 0`, it indicates that `(0, 0)` is one of the solutions & picking `0` will lead to it & the coefficient of the other variable is picked
    - else, `0` is picked for efficiency
2. resulting equation is solved for the other variable
3. values are written in the form of ordered pairs as their corresponding components

slope-intercept form of first degree linear equations in two variables: `y = mx + b`
- derived by isolation of `y`
- `(0, b)` is where y-intercept happens
- `m` is slope

point-slope form of first degree linear equations in two variables: `m(x - x_1) = (y - y_1)`
- calculated upon either two unknown points or one known point & slope of equation
- `(x_1, x_2)` is a known solution
- `m` is slope

parallel lines: lines which have the same slope & never intersect.<br>
perpendicular lines: lines which intersect at 90 degree angles.<br>
product of slopes of two non vertical perpendicular lines is equal to `-1`.

relations: sets of ordered pairs.<br>
functions: relations in which every unique `x` corresponds to exactly one single `y`.<br>
domain of functions: set of all `x`s in their relations.<br>
range of functions: set of all `y`s in their relations.

equations can be used to mathematically model relationships between two variables in the form of functions.<br>
vertical line test: determines if equations are functions by examining if their graph intersects more than once with any vertical line.

systems of linear equations: multiple equations in the same set of values
- inconsistent: made of parallel lines & don't have solutions
- consistent: made of independent equations (having one intersection) & has one solution
- made of depndent equations (with infinite intersections): has infinite solutions

methods to solve systems of linear equations in two variables
- substitution
    1. one of the variables is isolated in one of the equations
    2. the variable is replaced by resulting expression in another equation
    3. the modified equation is solved for the remaining variable
        - if this step results in false equation it indicates that the system is inconsistent
        - if this step causes the variable to drop out it means the system is made of dependent equations
    4. the variable is replaced by resulting value in either of equations
    5. the modified equation is solved for the remaining variable
    6. values are written in the form of ordered pairs as their corresponding components
- elimination
    1. equations are transformed into their standard form
    2. equations are manipulated such that one pair of variable terms are additive inverses among equations
    3. equations are merged together on both sides
    4. resulting equation is solved for the remaining variable
    5. the variable is replaced by resulting value in either of equations
    6. the modified equation is solved for the remaining variable
    7. values are written in the form of ordered pairs as their corresponding components

`(a / b)^n = b / a^(-n)`

polynomials: expressions containing finite number of terms with real coefficients & integers exponents greater than or equal to `0`
- monomials: contain one term
- binomials: contain two terms
- trinomials: contain three terms

`a * m^0 = a`<br>
`a + 0b = a`

degree of term: sum of exponents of their variables.<br>
degree of expressions (or polynomials): highest degree among their non-zero terms.<br>
terms are ordered by their degrees in standard form of polynomials.<br>

`a(b + c) = a * b + a * c`<br>
`(a + b)/c = a / c + b / c`<br>
FOIL method for multiplication of binomials given `(a + b)(c + d)`
1. (f)irst: `a * c`
2. (o)uter: `a * d`
3. (i)nner: `b * c`
4. (l)ast: `b * d`
5. results are merged

special products of polynomials with pre-defined answers, recognizable patterns & reversibility
- square of binomials
    - `(a + b)^2 = a^2 + 2ab + b^2`
    - `(a - b)^2 = a^2 - 2ab + b^2`
- cube of binomials
    - `(a + b)^3 = a^3 + 3ba^2 + 3ab^2 + b^3`
    - `(a - b)^3 = a^3 - 3ba^2 + 3ab^2 - b^3`
- difference of squares: `(a + b)(a - b) = a^2 - b^2`

steps to divide polynomials (fingers crossed, idk if it works all of the time)
1. each polynomial is transformed into standard form & made sure to have all of its degrees (or exponents)
2. leading term of dividend is divided by the leading term of divisor
3. resulting term is added to the quotient & the quotient is transformed into standard form (if required)
4. resulting term is multiplied by the divisor & its result is subtracted from dividend
5. rest of the previous dividend is appended to the remainder to make new one
6. conditional step
    - if the new dividend is more than the divisor goto step 2
    - else, division is finished

greatest common factor (GCF) aka greatest common divisor (GCD)
- largest positive term which devides given terms
- product of all common prime factors with the least repetition among given terms (variables are treated as prime factors)

steps to simplify polynomials by grouping
- used when division by GCF of their terms deosn't simplify them enough
1. terms are grouped such that division by their GCF results in common expressions
2. each group is simplified separately
3. resulting expression is divided by common expressions

`a * b = 0` therefore, `a = 0` or `b = 0`.

quadratic equations or inequations (in `x`)
- their overall form is `ax^2 + bx + c = 0`
- `a != 0`

steps to simplify trinomials of quadratic equations through unFOIL
1. trinomial is divided by GCF of its terms
2. two integers `m` & `n` are found where `a * c = m * n` & `b = m + n`
    - if not possible, the trinomial is prime
3. trinomial is written in the form of `ax^2 + mx + nx + c = 0`
4. resulting expression is simplified through grouping

methods to solve quadratic equations
- reversing square of binomials special product
- factoring
    1. the equation is transformed into product of terms being equal to `0`
        - this can be done through factoring, grouping, unFOIL, reversing special products or combination of these methods
        - if this can't be done the equation is prime & can't be solved through this method
    2. each reuslting term is written as being equal to `0`
    3. resulting equations are solved
    4. answers which don't satisfy the original equation are excluded
- completing the square
    1. constant term is isolated
    2. the equation is manipulated such that `a = 1`
    3. `(b / 2)^2` is added to both sides of the equation
    4. resulting equation is solved through reversing squre of binomials special product
- quadratic formula
    - `discriminant` is calculated as `b^2 - 4ac`
    - if `discriminant < 0`, the equation doesn't have real solutions
    - if `discriminant = 0`, the equation has one solution
    - if `discriminant > 0`, the equation has two solutions
    - solutions are calculated as `(-b + radical_2(discriminant)) / 2a` & `(-b - radical_2(discriminant)) / 2a`

rational expressions: quotients of polynomials where their denominators is not equal to `0`.<br>
restricted values of rational expressions
- substitutions resulting in their denominators being equal to `0` or being non-rational (or non real)
- calculated by solving their denominators for `0`

rational expressions are simplified by simplification of their numerators & denominators & dropping out common factors between them.<br>
solutions of equations or inequations containing rational expressions within their restricted values are rejected.<br>

variations: used to model changes of related variables.<br>
constant of variations: defines amount of change of depended variables as their dependents change; they act as slope but are usually annotated as `k`.

direct variations
- their overall form is `y = kx^n`
- `n` & `k` are constants
- `x` & `y` vary directly if `k > 0`, otherwise, inversely

inverse variations
- their overall form is `y = k/x^n`
- `n` & `k` are constants
- `x` & `y` vary inversely if `k > 0`, otherwise, directly

`radical_n(m) = x` where `x^n = m` (`n = 2` if not specified).<br>
given `radical_n(m)` where `n` is even
- two roots are resulted, one of which is negative & the other one is positive (called principal root)
- if `m` is negative, resulted roots aren't real numbers (can be presented using imaginary numbers)

pythagoras' theorm given right triangles having `a` & `b` as legs of their right angles & `c` as their third leg
- `a^2 + b^2 = c^2`
- used to calculated distance between two points on the cartesian chart `radical_2((x2 - x1)^2 + (y2 - y1)^2)`

`radical_n(m^x) = m^(x / n)`<br>
`radical(a / b) = radical(a) / radical(b)`<br>
`radical(a * b) = radical(a) * radical(b)`<br>

perfect roots: factors which their exponents are equal or greater than indices of radicals.<br>
simplified radicals: radicals which don't have fractions & perfect factors within their operands.<br>
like radicals: radicals which sharing the same index & operand; their combination only affects their coefficients.<br>

conjugate: same set of given combination of terms combined using different signs.<br>

methods to remove non real roots from denominators of rational expressions
- multiplication of their numerators & denominators by the irrational factor itself
    - takes place `n` times where `n` is the index of the irrational radical
    - is done after simplification
    - works best if there is one irrational factor
- multiplication of their numerators & denominator by the smallest root which makes the irrational factor rational
- multiplication of their numerators & denominator by the conjugate of their denominator (works best if the denominator consists of two terms)

both sides of equations containing radicals can be raised to their indices (after isolation & simplification) in order to be simplified with the side effect of extra answers.
